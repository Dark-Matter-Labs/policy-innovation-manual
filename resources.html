06 · The Navigation Kit

The Navigation
Kit

A set of entry points where cities can leverage Mission-Driven Policy Innovation to advance their mission goals. The Kit provides a shared language, a process to move through, and a set of working canvases — designed to be co-designed with each city, not applied off the shelf.

Audience
City Transition Teams
Deployed via
City Advisors & City Support Groups
Form
Living, co-designed tools

The ideaWhat Mission-Driven Policy Innovation is

Three ideas combine into one working definition.

Mission

The audacious societal challenge, translated into a city-specific context: climate neutral and smart by 2030.

+

Policy

The what — legislative, financial, and information & convening instruments — and the how: the processes by which policy is made.

+

Innovation

Novel ideas and methods that create effective policy interventions, and accelerate and amplify their effect.

Mission-Driven Policy Innovation Using innovative, effective policy interventions to accelerate and amplify a city's trajectory towards becoming climate neutral, smart and just by 2030.

Entry points, not answers

The Kit is designed to give cities a place to start. As entry points, these tools help a city team identify where policy support could help and where to begin, provide a starting point for co-designing city-responsive assets, consolidate best practice into shareable learning, open a gateway to policy settings such as workshops, labs and sandboxes, build off the transition map and existing programmes, and act as conversation tools for convening stakeholders.

A note on how they are used The tools are designed to be co-designed with cities and to flex to context — some cities need a simplified version, others want more complexity. They provide a scaffold and a shared vocabulary; each city works through them at the level of resolution that fits.

The processThe five practices

Mission-Driven Policy Innovation moves through five practices. In reality the process is non-linear — teams pull tools from different practices and simplify or deepen them as needed — but each practice answers a distinct question, and each connects to the wider NetZeroCities programme.

Practice 01

Strategic agenda setting

Do we have the legitimacy
to advance this agenda?

What

  • Reflect on how the mission mandate gives direction, justification and legitimacy for a policy agenda
  • Create visibility of the policy actors needed to co-design it
  • Observe the governance culture that helps or hinders the mission

How

Cities reflect on how strong their mandate and chosen priorities are — considering the stakeholders involved, the scale of ambition, and the clarity of direction communicated in the mandate.

Who

City Transition Teams with policy makers and stakeholders. Connects to the Climate Transition Map, the Climate City Contract, and transition-team development.

Why

MDPI starts with a co-created policy agenda that defines the scope and ambition of the transition, and strengthens the legitimacy of the interventions needed to meet it.

Default policy-making
  • Input from the usual policy makers, lobbyists and experts
  • Problem-oriented, reactive mindset
  • Reducing the challenge into parts to solve
  • Single-point levers as the intended outcome
→
Mission-driven practice
  • Broad input and output, across departments and levels of government
  • Vision-oriented, proactive mindset
  • Understanding the challenge holistically and systemically
  • A multiplicity of interventions as the pathway
Practice 02

Understanding the landscape

Do we have visibility of
existing policies?

What

  • Identify the wicked problems in the city's impact pathway
  • Build a systemic, de-siloed reading of the existing policy landscape
  • Set the policy objectives and learning enquiries to frame the work

How

An entry point into exploring how MDPI could deploy into the city's existing impact pathway and transition map — sequenced towards a shared policy innovation brief.

Who

Policy makers and stakeholders together, building shared understanding. Connects to the Climate City Contract (impact pathway 'pinch points') and the Climate Transition Map.

Why

The work needs deeper visibility of the city's existing trajectory, the wicked problems within it, and the potential impact of combined policy positions.

Default policy-making
  • Over-reliance on input from experts and policy makers
  • A standard, linear process of research and analysis
  • A focus on the symptoms, or on a single root cause
→
Mission-driven practice
  • Valuing input from a broader range of stakeholders for shared clarity
  • Collectively sensemaking the landscape and its trajectories
  • A focus on root causes, feedback loops and leverage points
Practice 03

Curating & orchestrating

What is in our policy
instrument inventory?

What

  • Articulate the leverage points and functions of policy instruments
  • Assess what stays, goes, improves or is added in the existing mix
  • Weigh candidate mixes against ex-ante criteria: desirability, feasibility, viability

How

Entry points for considering what functions a mix needs, what already exists in the instrument inventory, and what to change or add — extending, where useful, to scenario-first or Three Horizons framings.

Who

Policy makers and stakeholders designing and testing new mixes. Connects to the Climate Transition Map (co-designing a portfolio), the Climate City Contract, and the Pilot City Programme.

Why

This practice reconciles the strategic agenda with the existing landscape by designing and testing a mix that aligns actors, actions and intents.

Default policy-making
  • Input through formal consultation only
  • A process shaped by budget constraints and standard cost-benefit analysis
  • A focus on single-point, market-fixing interventions
→
Mission-driven practice
  • Informal and formal input to confirm aligned interests and commitments
  • Coordinating actions to build political appetite and test dependencies
  • A portfolio of collective, cross-sector, co-beneficiary interventions
Practice 04

Formalising & implementing

How do we measure
& iterate?

What

  • Visualise the portfolio of actors and actions advancing the mix
  • Build a dynamic, multi-layered evaluation framework
  • Institutionalise practices — scale up, out and deep — and iterate the policy cycle itself

How

A continuation of the ex-ante criteria from Practice 03, oriented toward managing, monitoring, learning and iterating the mix so it keeps advancing the policy goals.

Who

City Transition Teams with policy makers and stakeholders. Connects to the Climate Transition Map (take action; make it the new normal), the Climate City Contract Action Plan, and the Pilot City Programme.

Why

The work is long-term, reflexive and persistent; impacts may not be immediately visible. Monitoring, learning and iterating is what compounds actions into a shift in trajectory.

Default policy-making
  • Controlled input from the policy makers deploying it
  • A focus on political approval, a fixed plan and delegated roles
  • Implementing only what is within immediate control and easily measured
→
Mission-driven practice
  • Collective input and many-to-many deployment of the mix
  • A portfolio of harder and softer actions across an alliance of actors
  • Synergistic implementation with space to experiment, and MEL designed to enable it
Practice 05

Reflect & learn

Continuous —
across every practice

What

  • Capture what worked and what did not, across the whole process
  • Iterate the tools themselves and the way they are used
  • Build the capability and confidence of city teams to lead the work

How

Learning runs underneath all four practices rather than sitting only at the end — a loop that feeds each round of work back into the next.

Who

City teams, partners and NZC together — with learning made legible and transferable so other cities and national ecosystems can pick it up.

Why

The most durable outcome is a change in the capability and agency of city teams to work on policy challenges themselves — the practice, as much as the product.
