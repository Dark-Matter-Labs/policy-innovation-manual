Before you scope, not after
Practitioner annex How to use this playbook

The most important decisions — who you target, how you frame the invitation, what you promise participants, how you manage the EC relationship — are made months before a session opens. Getting those right is where this document earns its place. It is intended to be read before you scope your lab.

Part IWhat we were trying to do

An EU Policy Lab is a structured multi-actor dialogue designed to build vertical policy coherence: the alignment of action across EU, national, regional and local levels around a shared challenge. The goal is sense-making: helping cities understand what the EU landscape means for their specific situation, identify where the real blockers sit, and generate grounded input at a moment when it can land. Cities can already read the policy documents; the value is in working through them together.

The two 90-minute sessions are the visible tip of a lab. Beneath them sits everything that makes those sessions possible.Part I · the iceberg

What actually makes a lab work

Tie the invitation to a live, concrete problem

Broad thematic hooks recruit generalists. Specific problem framings recruit the people actually close to the problem — and the quality of conversation differs sharply.

Identification is not engagement

The gap between knowing who the right person is and getting their committed, prepared participation is what the runway exists to close. It cannot be compressed.

The facilitator is also a steward

Each facilitator owns a small group from pre-lab to post-lab — contacting participants, keeping them oriented, carrying the thread between sessions. This is relationship work as much as facilitation.

Clarity of purpose is internal before external

Where sessions drifted, the root cause was usually a lack of internal alignment about what the lab was actually for. Agree the primary purpose before designing anything else.

The structural tension to navigate EU partners often come to collect evidence — to hear from cities what needs to change. That is legitimate, and the Policy Briefs serve it. But when that extractive dynamic enters the working sessions — Commission officers in breakout rooms, conversation becoming an interview — cities lose the space to think together and arrive at specifics, and both sides get less than they came for. The resolution: the pre-lab briefing and post-lab reflection are the right spaces for direct city–Commission exchange. The working sessions belong to the cities.

Part IIIBefore you scope anything: five questions

The team needs to answer these before any design work begins. If you cannot answer them — or answer them differently from each other — that is the work to do first.

1

What is the specific policy window?

The precise moment when input can land: a consultation deadline, a transposition date, a communication expected in a given quarter. The sharper this is, the better the participants.

2

What is the primary purpose?

Choose one: diagnosing blockers, peer learning, evidence for an EC process, or building city agency to act nationally. Other things may also happen — but one must be primary.

3

Who specifically needs to be in the room?

Not "cities working on housing" — which person, in which role, with which relationship to this challenge. If you cannot name the profile, you cannot target effectively.

4

What does the EC relationship look like?

Who are your contacts, what do they want, and where will they appear? Agree upfront: working sessions belong to cities; Commission engagement happens in pre- and post-lab formats.

5

Are the three workflow streams covered?

Technical input & translation (owns the EU landscape and the Brief); participant journey & comms; and design & purpose (holds the arc). Named people, protected time — the accountability cannot be fuzzy.

The operational essentials, in brief

On targeting: use city advisers as active brokers — ask "who in this city is closest to this challenge? Can you introduce us?" Frame the invitation around the problem itself. Prepare the email a practitioner can forward to their manager to justify the time.

On facilitation: the steward model is essential — personalised contact before the briefing, being the named point of contact, a personalised recap after Session 1, a nudge during the mid-lab, a check-in before Session 2. Budget for it explicitly. Brief facilitators on why the Miro frames are sequenced as they are, not just the content.

On the Miro board: it only works if participants know what is coming. Share a PDF of the frames in advance; walk through the structure as well as the content in the pre-lab; run the first frame as a plenary. Keep the number of frames to what can be completed with depth. One frame done well beats four skimmed.

On the mid-lab week: make it specific or cut it. It only works with a bounded task and an active facilitator prompt — not an open invitation to reconvene.

On post-lab synthesis: the Policy Brief takes about two full days for a solid first draft. Protect that time before the lab starts, and make sure the writer was in every session. Send the draft to all participants before publication.

On the EC relationship: Commission officers are welcome at the pre-lab briefing and the post-lab reflection; they should not be in breakout rooms. Frame it as being in the Commission's interest — a working session that produces rich, grounded insight is more useful than a facilitated Q&A.

The runwayA timeline for each stage

The sprint — pre-lab, Session 1, mid-lab, Session 2, post-lab — is the visible structure. But the work that makes it possible starts ten to twelve weeks before the pre-lab briefing opens.

T-12 to T-8 wks

Scope & EC alignment

Arrive at a confirmed topic, a clear policy window, internal team alignment and EC buy-in — before any public communication goes out. Build in buffer; EC drafting timelines slip.

T-8 to T-4 wks

Targeting, outreach & registration

Get the right people genuinely committed and registered. Brief city advisers to recommend specific individuals; open registration only once dates are confirmed.

T-4 to T-2 wks

Materials, Miro & facilitator prep

Dossier drafted, Miro board built and tested by every facilitator, pre-lab deck ready, facilitator briefing held — walking through the facilitation logic as well as the content.

Sprint · 4–6 wks

Pre-lab → S1 → mid-lab → S2

A consistent, warm communication cadence around each touchpoint — the steward model in practice. Recordings within 24h; personalised group recaps within 48h.

Post-lab

Synthesis & staying connected

Two protected days for the Brief; participant review; submission to the DG; a mailing list for future updates; a team reflection; and this playbook updated with new learning.

Phase 1 checklist — Scope & EC alignment

  • Lab brief drafted: topic, policy window, why now, what cities get, what the EC gets
  • Brief shared with DG RTD and the relevant topic DG for input and alignment
  • EC feedback received; the policy window confirmed as real and well-timed
  • Co-lead partner(s) confirmed and onboarded to scope and purpose
  • Three workflow streams named: technical input, participant journey, lab design
  • Draft dates proposed for all sprint touchpoints
  • Previous lab Miro boards and Policy Briefs reviewed by anyone new to the team
