Within the tiered model, Task 6.5 uses four primary operational methods. These are not interchangeable — each has a distinct logic, format, appropriate use case, and set of preconditions. Choosing well, and knowing when to move between them, is itself a design skill.

Read across the axesThe methods at a glance

Each method belongs to a support level and answers to a different timescale and readiness. This is the tree read either way — down the methods, or across the support levels.

Policy Studio

Sustained · foundational · open-ended at the start

A Policy Studio is a sustained, foundational space — typically six months or more — in which a city or cluster works through the full arc from problem identification to coalition-building and early intervention design. The studio is deliberately open-ended at the start: it holds space for iterative, multi-stakeholder sensemaking before committing to a bounded direction.

Duration
6 months or more
Team
Strong core of decision-makers & cross-departmental representatives, plus external domain specialists
Preconditions
Appetite for exploratory working; institutional grounding
Primary example
Romania — M100 Innovation Studio
Deep support

Policy Lab

Bounded · focused · policy-mix development

A Policy Lab is a bounded, focused process that begins once the problem space is sufficiently understood — typically two to three months. Labs are oriented toward testing and developing specific policy instrument mixes rather than diagnosing the broader landscape. The transition from a studio to a lab is itself a significant design moment: it marks the point where open-ended sensemaking gives way to bounded policy development.

Duration
2–3 months
Team
Smaller than a studio; a well-scoped challenge
Preconditions
Crystallised problem; political backing for experimentation
Primary example
Finland — moved into a lab in phase three
Light support

Policy Lab Sprint

Compressed · tied to a policy window · EU-level

A Policy Lab Sprint is a compressed, four-to-six-week format designed for light-touch engagement tied to a specific, current policy window. Sprints create structured space for rapid knowledge exchange, challenge definition, and the development of early-stage policy asks — particularly at EU level, where legislative timelines create defined moments of opportunity. They work best when cities arrive with a reasonably settled problem definition and enough mandate to act.

Duration
4–6 weeks (extendable)
Team
Facilitator-stewards; a technical translator; comms infrastructure
Preconditions
A live, concrete policy window
Primary example
EU Policy Labs 1–3 · the Playbook
Deep support · national

Regulatory Sandbox

Controlled real-world testing · most resource-intensive

A Regulatory Sandbox provides a controlled space in which new regulatory approaches can be tested in real-world conditions before formal adoption. Establishing one requires national-level approval and delegation to a competent body — making it among the most resource-intensive and politically complex formats, and one that cannot be advanced by NZC or city teams alone.

Status in SGA1 No full sandbox was established within SGA1, though the framework has been developed and socialised through dedicated conference sessions. Potential leads have been identified in Ireland, Italy and Greece — and the Vilnius sandbox session generated immediate appetite from Cork, which expressed formal interest in establishing one.
Duration
Multi-year; national approval cycle
Requires
National-level approval; delegation to a competent body
Preconditions
National partner; political mandate; a testable regulatory question
Leads
Ireland (retrofit finance), Italy, Greece
A note on the fourth tier Integrated support does not map to a single method — it is the connective layer that surfaces challenges and builds the pipeline through CCC reviews, the learning programme, and one-to-one accompaniment. See the Support Model and the integrated portfolio.
