04 · Lessons & Direction

What the work
taught us

Cities' climate ambitions are less constrained by an absence of solutions than by the difficulty of navigating, aligning and reshaping policy frameworks across multiple levels of governance. The question was never whether policy innovation matters — it was how to make it possible in practice.

Cross-cutting lessonsPatterns that hold across the portfolio

These recur with enough consistency to be treated as structural learning — offered as hard-won evidence from practice.

Anchor in a challenge cities are already moving — then connect it structurally

The most effective entry point is the specific challenge a city is already trying to move. Romania showed this: anchoring the studio to cycling challenges cities were already spending political capital on secured buy-in. But those challenges are often structural and need connecting across governance levels first. This is a meeting in the middle — working from city-level investment upward toward the structural conditions that determine whether change is possible.

Effective partners are co-producers and brokers, not intermediaries

The work that produced the most traction depended on partners who brought mandate, local knowledge and relational access NZC could not replicate from outside — M100 in Romania, Demos Helsinki and Anna Huttunen in Finland. What matters is whether the partner brings genuine brokering capacity, whatever their organisational form.

Local diagnosis and national engagement must be connected

Local diagnosis reveals the technical and governance dimensions of a challenge; national engagement is required to address its regulatory, fiscal and institutional roots. Work that stays local risks well-mapped problems with no pathway to change; work that jumps to national level without grounded diagnosis risks generic advocacy. Vertical coherence is the core value-add.

The "problem behind the problem" is often governance — and shifting it needs senior engagement

The visible policy challenge is frequently a symptom of a deeper governance problem. Moving through the visible problem to the structural one requires time, trust and iterative diagnosis — but also senior-level engagement. A recurring constraint in SGA1 was the absence of a mechanism for brokering with mayors, senior officials and national ministries. Studios and labs can surface pathways; without senior leadership driving them, the work risks falling at the final hurdle.

Adaptive methodologies require diverse skills — and that is hard to resource

Delivering this work requires an unusual combination operating simultaneously across a sustained arc: process design, relational intelligence, policy depth, domain knowledge, multi-level governance understanding, and facilitation. Assembling and sustaining that capacity within a consortium has been a consistent structural constraint.

Building city agency matters as much as the outputs

Across all cases, the most durable outcome is a change in the capability and confidence of city teams to work on policy challenges themselves — more than any brief or decision map. In Finland, cities built the muscle to collectively prioritise; in Romania, Alba Iulia institutionalised cycling as a formal function. These shifts in agency are what the work is ultimately for.

Demand exceeds resources — the question is strategic use, not volume

After finance, policy innovation is the most commonly cited support need. The strategic question is how to use resources so they compound instead of dissipating — integrating policy perspectives into other workstreams and aligning with funded city programmes.

The direction of travelFive shifts into SGA3 & SGA4

1

From lever-led / demand-led → to challenge-led

A challenge-led organising logic

Traction comes when the work is anchored in a concrete, specific challenge cities recognise as live and consequential. SGA4 will formalise this as the primary organising logic — selecting a small number of mission-critical challenge spaces and structuring labs, sandboxes and national engagement around them.

2

From unfunded extracurricular → to structurally resourced

Aligned with funded programmes

Cities cannot prioritise the work unless staff time is covered. Finnish cities were interested for over a year before ECT pilot funding gave them a legitimate mandate to engage deeply. Policy innovation needs to be structurally aligned with programmes cities already work within — principally the Pilot City Programme and ECT2/ECT3 cascade funding.

3

From fragmented activity → to coherent multi-year pathways

Sequenced pathways, not one-offs

SGA1 produced a wide, valuable portfolio; the limitation was coherence between activities. The shift is toward fewer, sequenced, multi-year pathways where studios, labs, sprints and sandboxes become stages that build on one another toward tested policy alternatives.

4

The pipeline problem → surfacing & escalating quality challenges

Building the capacity to spot what matters

A structural gap SGA1 revealed: the capacity of city advisers and frontline staff to identify and escalate genuinely mission-critical policy challenges. The upstream work — shared language, diagnostic capability, frontline confidence — was not anticipated in the original design and needs explicit resourcing.

5

The product → is matched by the practice

The practice matters as much as the product

What actually shifts policy is the practice of policy-making: people from different levels and institutional cultures sitting together, within a bounded space, over a sustained period, learning to work toward something they could not reach independently. Making that practice legible and transferable — so cities and national ecosystems can increasingly lead it themselves — is a core ambition for the next phase.

More than a record of finished projects, this is the evidence base, the methodology, and the network of relationships on which the next phase can build. Conclusion · D6.7
