Finland — Urban Logistics & Transport Policy Lab

Six Finnish Mission Cities · Oct 2025 – Jun 2026

Deep Studio → Lab

Transport and urban logistics are among the most persistent sources of emissions in Finnish cities. Progress is structurally difficult: these systems cut across municipal, regional and national competences. What the cities needed was alignment: a shared baseline, clarity on where challenges genuinely overlapped, and a working understanding of who should do what beyond any single municipality. The technical knowledge largely existed already.

The engagement began as a Policy Studio — open-ended sensemaking and policy mapping across six cities — and transitioned into a Policy Lab once the problem crystallised around financing and sustaining new logistics practices, and the ECT projects in Lahti and Helsinki provided institutional grounding. Two frameworks shaped the work: Humble Governance (thin consensus around broad goals, peer learning as feedback) and Portfolio Mapping (surfacing existing policies to find gaps and co-investment opportunities).

Outputs & shifts

  • Shared challenge briefs across six cities; a multi-level stakeholder map; a portfolio map of existing policies with identified gaps
  • Co-designed policy-mix options, tested through stakeholder dialogue and foresight scenarios
  • A distributed-ownership model — lead cities holding specific policy sub-areas by genuine interest, not assignment
  • A nascent working coalition in place of parallel city-level work; Lahti and Helsinki positioned as anchor cities
Grey learnings Practitioner reflections from Finland ›
  • Hold the format lightly at the start. The studio-to-lab transition was not planned — it emerged as the problem crystallised. Committing too early to a bounded lab would have foreclosed the sensemaking that made policy-mix development possible.
  • Multiple motivations are an asset if held carefully. Cities joined for different reasons — ECT linkages, SUMP/SULP iteration, mayoral priorities, liveability. Holding that diversity without flattening it into false consensus was a defining facilitation challenge.
  • Trust capital cannot be manufactured on short timelines. Demos Helsinki's and Anna Huttunen's existing credibility were load-bearing from the start.
  • In later phases, the relational infrastructure becomes the primary work. Ensuring clear, motivated, empowered owners as NZC stepped back mattered more than policy content.
  • Engage both domain experts and Mission Coordinators. Only experts risked isolation; only coordinators risked the work becoming too general to act on.

Romania — M100 Innovation Studio on Cycling Infrastructure

Bucharest & Alba Iulia · Sep 2025 – Jun 2026

Deep Studio

In Romania's cities the barrier to cycling infrastructure is governance, more than technology or finance. In Bucharest, a single bike lane can require clearances from five to fifteen institutions, and the Traffic Police hold effective veto power. The studio's most significant output from Bucharest was a map: of who holds what power, where the veto points sit, and what workarounds exist within the current legal architecture.

The work was initiated through and with Romania's M100 National Platform — the enabler without which NZC could not have convened these actors. Alba Iulia moved faster into action: a weekly school-cycling pilot, a mandated cycling team institutionalised within the municipality, and a private-sector co-financing model now moving into practical testing. Running across both cities is a national cycling policy brief, deliberately politically neutral so it survives political turbulence — a design choice that proved its worth when the national government collapsed in May 2026.

Outputs & shifts

  • Decision-pathway map of Bucharest's cycling governance — veto points, coordination gaps, institutional workarounds
  • First documented cross-sector dialogue between PMB and Sector 2, resulting in a joint funding mechanism
  • Cycling given an institutionalised home in Alba Iulia for the first time
  • National cycling policy brief in development — technically grounded, resilient to political turbulence
Grey learnings Practitioner reflections from Romania ›
  • Romanian cities are not built for emergence. The studio's exploratory logic sat uncomfortably against a strong preference for fixed deliverables. Maintaining engagement required ongoing translation into the language of concrete outputs.
  • The national platform is the work itself. M100's legitimacy and Maria's active co-facilitation were load-bearing from the start. This model cannot be replicated without an equivalent embedded national partner.
  • Governance complexity is the object of study, as much as a barrier. The decision-pathway map is now the foundation for everything that follows.
  • Political instability changes what is possible, not what is needed. The pivot to technical dialogues after the government collapse was preparation, not retreat.
  • You cannot parachute generic European policy advice into a post-communist, highly siloed context. Reini's ministry connections and Eugen's administrative knowledge were what made the national dimension credible.

Vitoria-Gasteiz & Madrid — Spatial Planning

Spain · Delivered (via City Expert Support Facility)

Deep Studio

A successful deep engagement supporting both cities to align their General Plans with Mission goals. The City Expert Support Facility (CESF) was deployed to bring in specific legal and planning expertise — the technical depth city teams needed but could not source internally. A strong example of what targeted expert deployment achieves when city appetite, mandate, and a well-defined challenge are all in place, and a model for how the CESF can function as a deep-support mechanism.

EU Policy Lab Sprints

EU-level · Oct 2025 – Winter 2026 · three labs

Light Sprint

A replicable methodology for structured multi-actor dialogue, connecting cities, national authorities and EU institutions around shared challenges at moments when engagement can directly inform EU policy. The core purpose is vertical policy coherence: clarifying what is in place — and what is needed — at each level, and creating conditions for grounded input into policy processes. Lab Sprints are working sessions — hands-on and collaborative — oriented toward a tangible output: a Policy Brief submitted to the relevant Directorate-General.

Lab 1 · complete

Heating & Cooling

Timed to the Commission's Heating & Cooling Strategy and Electrification Action Plan consultations. Brief submitted to DG ENER.

Lab 2 · finalising

Gas Package Transposition

DSO decommissioning schedules and stranded-asset risk ahead of the July 2026 transposition deadline.

Lab 3 · ongoing

Affordable & Sustainable Housing

Tied to the European Affordable Housing Plan. Extended into an eight-to-nine-month, multi-phase engagement.

The sprint methodology

  • Pre-Lab — a 45–60 min briefing and a curated dossier making the EU landscape legible in city-relevant terms
  • Session 1 — vertical mapping across EU, national and local levels; surfacing the real blockers
  • Mid-Lab — a bounded task (challenge sheets) for city-led information gathering
  • Session 2 — refining analysis into specific policy asks
  • Post-Lab — consolidation into a formal Policy Brief and a reflection session

The full operational method — the runway, the Miro architecture, the facilitation model — is documented in the EU Policy Labs Playbook.

Grey learnings Practitioner reflections across the three labs ›
  • The pre-lab phase is the most determinative factor in output quality — and the least visible. Specific problem framing, the right participants, and dossier depth almost entirely determined what happened in the room.
  • The format works best tied to a live policy window. A consultation open, a directive in transposition, a plan just launched — that is when engagement sharpens and the brief carries weight.
  • Two-track engagement helps the breadth–depth tension. Letting participants choose headline awareness or full working engagement served a wide audience without diluting the substantive work.
  • Consortium co-delivery is a strength and a coordination cost. Breadth of networks and expertise, but real coordination investment between sessions.
  • The housing lab revealed the limits of the sprint format for politically complex agendas. Extending Lab 3 into a multi-phase engagement was the right call — knowing when to extend is itself a design judgement.

Poland — Municipal Finance & Debt Ceilings

Poland · Scoped & started

Deep (scoped) Studio

A Policy Studio focused on the debt-ceiling rules limiting cities' ability to invest in climate transition, scoped and initiated with a City Finance Specialist. The challenge is well-defined and the need significant. City-capacity constraints prevented the cohort from maintaining collective alignment — advancing it requires dedicated support for city participation and trust-building with finance departments and treasuries. The diagnostic work is strong and the design for a resumed studio is clear.

Ireland — Regulatory Sandbox for Retrofit Finance

Cork & Dublin · Consensus-building

Deep (proposed) Sandbox

Following the regulatory-sandbox workshop in Vilnius, conversations with Cork and Dublin identified a clear opportunity: a sandbox exploring funding-eligibility requirements for the retrofitting of public buildings. City appetite appeared strong and the direction was scoped for the Irish Mirror Group to consider — connecting directly to the Dublin cross-council retrofit-licensing work.

The Integrated Portfolio

EU-wide · woven through the NZC programme

Integrated

The integrated layer was not formally briefed or resourced within Task 6.5. It emerged from a recognition that a coherent portfolio requires presence, continuity and trust across the city network, beyond isolated interventions. With minimal dedicated resourcing, the team wove policy support through the wider NZC architecture. It should be understood as foundational infrastructure for everything else described here.

Where it showed up

  • CCC Reviews — policy innovation embedded into the Climate City Contract review process, with a shared deck and worksheet for other reviewers
  • Seasonal School, Milan (Jun 2025) — a scenario-based simulation in the fictional city of "Solevento", shifting how participants understood policy design
  • Online Learning Programme (Nov–Dec 2025) — "Mission-Minded Cities": a shared vocabulary for Studios, Labs and Sandboxes
  • Vilnius Conference — sessions on regulatory sandboxes (prompting Cork's interest) and on the policy–finance intersection
  • City-level work — Greek cities (Kozani, Ioannina, Thessaloniki), Hungarian cities (Budapest, Miskolc, Pécs), Guimarães, and the four Dublin councils
Grey learnings Cross-portfolio reflections ›
  • Scoping is the first step — the critical shift comes after. Surfacing that Italy needs a data-governance intervention has value; the moment that matters most is proposing pathways and co-designing how cities and NZC move through it together.
  • The hidden layer is consensus and mandate-building. Before substantive work can begin, cities and platforms must agree a shared scope and the mandate to act. Rarely budgeted for; determines whether everything else is possible.
  • Without dedicated funding for city staff time, even strong appetite has a ceiling. Guimarães, Poland and Hungary all illustrate this — goodwill is not a substitute for attributed time.
  • National brokering relationships are a pre-condition, not a by-product. Without pre-existing trust at platform level, the facilitation load falls on NZC and progress slows.
  • This work was delivered with minimal dedicated resourcing — and that has a cost. The gap between what was attempted and what was resourced is itself a signal.
